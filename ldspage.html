Learning Disabilities

Learning disabilities are a group of neurodevelopmental disorders that can significantly hamper a person’s ability to learn new things. These disorders involve difficulty in one or more, but not uniformly in all, basic psychological processes:

  1. Input (auditory and visual perception): Processing information by visual and auditory means, which may impact reading, spelling, writing, and understanding or using language.

  2. Integration (sequencing, abstraction, and organization): Prioritizing, organizing, doing mathematics, and following instructions.

  3. Memory (working, short-term, and long-term memory): Storing or retrieving information from short or long-term memory.

  4. Output (expressive language): Using spoken language. Some people who struggle with talking might use alternative communication methods like sign language, Signalong, Makaton, or digital systems like Picture Exchange Communication Systems (PECS) or Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC).

  5. Motor (fine and gross motor): Clumsiness or difficulty with handwriting.

Types of Learning Disabilities

“Learning disability” is an umbrella term encompassing many specific learning disorders, including:

  • Dyslexia: The most common learning disability, characterized by difficulty with speaking, reading, writing, or understanding words. This can cause slower vocabulary development, grammar issues, and reading comprehension difficulties.

  • Dysgraphia: Difficulty putting thoughts into writing due to challenges with vocabulary, spelling, grammar, memory, and motor planning. Poor handwriting and difficulty thinking while writing are common.

  • Dyscalculia: Also known as “math dyslexia,” it involves challenges with numbers, mathematical concepts, and reasoning. Students may struggle with counting money, telling time, performing calculations, or identifying number patterns.

  • Auditory Processing Disorder (APD): Difficulty processing sounds, leading to confusion in the order of sounds, misinterpreting words, or trouble distinguishing important sounds from background noise.

  • Language Processing Disorder (LPD): A subset of APD, affecting the ability to attach meaning to sound groups representing words, sentences, and stories.

  • Nonverbal Learning Disabilities (NVLD): Difficulty interpreting nonverbal cues like facial expressions, body language, tone of voice, and other signals.

  • Visual Perceptual/Visual Motor Deficit: Challenges with hand-eye coordination and motor activities. Common signs include losing one's spot while reading, confusing similar letters, or struggling with writing and navigating the environment.

Accommodations and Modifications

Success in the classroom often depends on appropriate adaptations, accommodations, and modifications:

  • Accommodations: Remove learning barriers to provide equal access. Examples include audio versions of text for students with dyslexia or visual supports for students with ASD. Tools, materials, technology, visual aids, physical space, and timing help the student access the curriculum.

  • Modifications: Change what a student is taught or expected to do. For example, a student struggling with spelling may be tested on a different set of words. Tools, materials, technology, visual aids, physical space, and timing are used to help the student achieve curriculum goals.

Each accommodation should match the individual student’s needs. Consider:

  • Which accommodations are needed to ensure equal footing?

  • Is there evidence supporting the accommodation?

  • Are accommodations linked to the student’s functional limitations?

Accommodations are effective only if the student knows how to use them. Extra time alone rarely improves performance; students must be taught strategies to use additional time effectively. Practice is key, and accommodations should be integrated into daily classroom routines before assessment use.
