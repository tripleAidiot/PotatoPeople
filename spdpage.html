Sensory Processing Disorder

What is sensory processing disorder?

Sensory processing disorder (SPD) is a difference in the way your brain understands and responds to information from your senses. This includes what you see, hear, smell, taste and touch. It can also affect your movement.

Healthcare providers and parents typically identify SPD in children. But it can also affect adults. SPD commonly affects people with neurodevelopmental and neurodivergent conditions, like autism spectrum disorder and attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). But you can also have SPD without any other condition.

Currently, SPD isn't an official medical diagnosis. But providers recognize the benefits of identifying SPD and using therapy to manage it, especially if it affects your ability to function day to day. Occupational therapists commonly see and treat children and adults with sensory processing problems.



Types of Sensory Processing Disorder

There are different types of sensory processing disorders, including:

  • Sensory Over-Responsivity / Hyper Sensitivity
  • Sensory Under-Responsivity / Hypo Sensitivity
  • Sensory Craving
  • Sensory Discrimination Disorder
  • Postural Disorder
  • Dyspraxia
Sensory Over-Responsivity (SOR) / Hyper Sensitivity

Here, you need far less sensory information than usual for it to have a profound impact. You may feel easily overwhelmed, distressed, or physically uncomfortable from ordinary sensory information. You may actively avoid certain inputs, become highly defensive, or experience a "fight-or-flight" reaction to daily stimuli.

Tactile (Touch)
  • Distressed by textures: Expresses intense discomfort or refuses to wear certain fabrics, itchy tags, or stiff clothing.

  • Avoids getting messy: Distressed by having sticky hands, mud, glue, or paint on the skin, wanting to wipe it off immediately.

  • Exaggerated pain response: Reacts with extreme distress to minor scrapes, tiny cuts, or gentle medical injections.

  • Dislikes unexpected contact: Pulls away, flinches, or reacts aggressively when touched gently or brushed against from behind.

  • Intolerant of grooming: Experiences intense dread or panic during hair cutting, nail clipping, face washing, or teeth brushing.

Auditory (Hearing)
  • Distressed by sudden noises: Covers ears or panics at everyday sounds like flushing toilets, vacuum cleaners, sirens, or clapping.

  • Distracted by background hums: Constantly notices and complains about low noises others ignore, like a refrigerator or AC unit.

  • Overwhelmed in crowds: Becomes irritable, anxious, or completely shuts down in noisy, echoing environments like cafeterias or malls.

  • Inability to screen out sounds: Cannot focus on a single speaker if there is any other faint conversation or noise happening in the room.

  • Demands total quiet: Frequently asks others to lower their voices, mute electronics, or stop making repetitive minor sounds (like ticking clocks).

Visual (Sight)
  • Extremely sensitive to light: Squints, covers eyes, or gets headaches from normal sunlight, fluorescent office lighting, or bright screens.

  • Overstimulated by clutter: Becomes anxious, distracted, or visually overwhelmed in rooms with busy wall decor, messy shelves, or bright patterns.

  • Avoids direct eye contact: Finds looking directly at faces or eyes intensely overwhelming or physically uncomfortable.

  • Triggered by movement: Easily distracted or agitated by moving objects in peripheral vision, like a ceiling fan or passing cars.

  • Shields vision: Frequently wears sunglasses indoors, pulls hats low, or covers eyes to block out standard room lighting.

Gustatory & Olfactory (Taste & Smell)
  • Extremely picky eater: Restricts diet to very specific textures or bland foods, gagging on mixed textures, spices, or certain ingredients.

  • Heightened smell detection: Notices subtle odors long before anyone else, often feeling nauseated by faint smells.

  • Gags at ordinary scents: Experiences intense physical aversion or gagging from perfumes, scented candles, cleaning chemicals, or cooking food.

  • Avoids specific locations: Refuses to enter specific environments (like fish markets, public bathrooms, or restaurants) due to concentrated odors.

  • Extreme temperature sensitivity: Refuses to eat or drink anything unless it has cooled down completely or reached an exact, mild temperature.

Vestibular & Proprioceptive (Movement & Body Position)
  • Fear of unstable surfaces: Becomes highly anxious or refuses to walk on uneven ground, thick grass, sand, or slippery tiles.

  • Motion sickness: Experiences nausea, dizziness, or intense anxiety from swings, elevators, moving cars, or escalators.

  • Fear of feet leaving ground: Panics when tipped backward, lifted, or when participating in activities where feet are suspended.

  • Protective body positioning: Moves rigidly and cautiously, often holding onto walls or handrails to avoid any risk of falling.

  • Dislikes rough play: Actively avoids any fast-moving games, tumbling, spinning, or unpredictable physical activities.

Sensory Under-Responsivity (SUR) / Hypo Sensitivity

Here, you need more sensory information than usual for it to have an impact. You may be unaware of sensory information and/or have a delay before responding, compared to most people. You may purposely seek out sensory input (sensory craving).

Tactile (Touch)
  • Does not notice when hands, face, or clothes are dirty, wet, or sticky.

  • Shows remarkably little reaction to painful cuts, scrapes, bruises, or injections.

  • Fails to notice if someone touches them gently on the shoulder or arm from behind.

  • Can wear shoes on the wrong feet or clothing twisted inside-out without noticing.

  • Doesn't register temperature changes, often ignoring extreme cold or heat.

Auditory (Hearing)
  • Fails to respond when their name is called, looking like they are ignoring you.

  • Needs verbal instructions repeated multiple times before taking any action.

  • Appears oblivious to loud, sudden background noises that startle other people.

  • Speaks in an unusually quiet voice or doesn't realize how loud they are being.

  • Appears to prefer a completely silent environment, rarely initiating sounds or talk.

Visual (Sight)
  • Stares blankly into space for long periods, appearing checked out.

  • Frequently misses objects directly in front of them or fails to notice new room decor.

  • Struggles to track moving objects visually (e.g., catching a slow-moving ball).

  • Misses written details on a whiteboard, page, or signs in their direct path.

  • Finds it difficult to distinguish between similar colors, shapes, or hidden figures.

Gustatory & Olfactory (Taste & Smell)
  • Prefers extremely bland foods and shows zero interest in spices or flavor variety.

  • Cannot distinguish between different tastes or identify what they are eating blindfolded.

  • Fails to notice strong odors like smoke, gas, spoiled food, or heavy perfumes.

  • Frequently eats spoiled or dangerously hot/cold food without noticing the issue.

  • Fails to smell cleaning chemicals or soaps that make others cough or sneeze.

Vestibular & Proprioceptive (Movement & Body Position)
  • Appears sluggish, lethargic, or slumps heavily over a desk or table while sitting.

  • Frequently drops items, trips over flat surfaces, or bumps into doorways.

  • Fails to notice when their body is slipping off a chair or tilting off balance.

  • Seems to have weak muscles, frequently complaining of being "too tired" to stand up.

  • Struggles to learn new physical coordinates or sequences (e.g., riding a bike).

Sensory Craving (SC)

In this type, you need and seek out sensory information for stimulation. But getting the stimulation results in disorganization and doesn't satisfy the craving.

Tactile (Touch)
  • Constantly touches everything and everyone, even when told it is inappropriate.

  • Rubs hands obsessively over textures like velvet, rough surfaces, or hair.

  • Prefers tight, heavy clothing, thick blankets, or squeezed hugs to feel grounded.

  • Intentionally seeks out messy play like mud, shaving cream, and sticky glue.

  • Rubs skin aggressively against walls, furniture, or carpets for tactile friction.

Auditory (Hearing)
  • Turns up television, tablet, or music volumes to the maximum level possible.

  • Constantly makes loud noises, hums, chants, or screams to fill silent spaces.

  • Seeks out chaotic, noisy environments like arcade rooms or crowded festivals.

  • Intentionally bangs doors, drops objects, or slams toys together to hear the impact.

  • Prefers listening to repetitive, heavy bass lines or fast, jarring auditory tracks.

Visual (Sight)
  • Stares directly into bright lights, spinning fans, or moving screens for hours.

  • Is deeply attracted to vibrant, flashing, neon, or rapidly shifting color displays.

  • Moves fingers, strings, or objects rapidly back and forth right in front of their eyes.

  • Loves looking at complex geometric patterns, visually cluttered layouts, or optical illusions.

  • Constantly seeks out visually chaotic environments like laser tag arenas or time square setups.

Gustatory & Olfactory (Taste & Smell)
  • Constantly craves intensely spicy, sour, salty, or burning hot/ice-cold foods.

  • Frequently licks or chews on non-food objects like shirt collars, pens, or toys.

  • Deliberately smells items like markers, gasoline, dirty shoes, or garbage bins.

  • Stuffs their mouth entirely full of food beyond a safe swallowing capacity.

  • Prefers highly carbonated, fizzy drinks or foods with highly abrasive crunch.

Vestibular & Proprioceptive (Movement & Body Position)
  • Constantly spins in circles, swings aggressively, or hangs upside down without getting dizzy.

  • Frequently jumps off furniture, crashes into walls, or wrestles aggressively.

  • Stomps feet heavily when walking instead of stepping softly.

  • Cannot sit still, continuously rocking, pacing, fidgeting, or shifting weight.

  • Purposefully takes dangerous physical risks on playground structures or heights.

Sensory Discrimination Disorder

This type includes difficulty interpreting subtle qualities of sensory information. There are several subtypes. For example, auditory discrimination disorder makes it difficult to understand the things you hear.

Tactile Domain

Children with tactile discrimination difficulties typically have difficulty determining what they touch "by feel"; they must see it (stereognosis). We use stereognosis many times a day. We just don't think about it because it comes to us automatically.

For example:

  • Buttoning a shirt or pants while holding cell phone to ear.

  • Touching something got out of microwave to know if it's the right temperature.

Proprioceptive Domain

How do we choose "just the right amount" of tension or force required to interact with an object (e.g., how hard to lift to carry box up the stairs)? It is our proprioceptors that give us the information about how hard our muscles have to contract to accomplish the box-lifting task.

If a child demonstrates difficulty in this area, he may show some of the following problems:

  • Roughhousing to the point of someone getting hurt.

  • Judging how much force to use when throwing a ball.

  • Using gentle touch to pet an animal.

Vestibular Domain

"The vestibular system helps determine where our head is in space, relative to gravity. If a child has difficulty with vestibular discrimination, he may not feel when he is starting to fall and will not be able to catch himself before he gets hurt."

For example:

  • Poor awareness of movement of body in space (gets disoriented easily).

  • Knows he's falling, but can't tell which way and can't protect himself.

Auditory Domain

Children with auditory discrimination challenges may be mislabeled with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) or disciplined for "never listening" because they appear to ignore what is said to them.

It may be difficult for them to hear the difference between background noise and the teacher asking, "Take out your science books and turn to page 103."

Symptoms may include:

  • Talks too loudly or softly.

  • Experiences confusion when given directions.

  • Appears to ignore others.

Visual Domain

Children with poor visual discrimination tend to have trouble in school. It may be difficult for them to "read" emotions or to recognize letters and symbols.

Other symptoms may include difficulty with:

  • Lining up numbers in a math problem.

  • Scanning a page to find the keywords in a story.

  • Judging the distance between oneself and an object or person.

Olfactory/Gustatory Domain

Smell and taste discrimination do not cause as severe a social problem, so they are usually only treated when they occur with another sensory discrimination issue.

For example:

  • Difficulty telling the difference between things that are somewhat sweet and those that are too sweet.

  • Cannot tell if bread is burning, but has general sense something is wrong.

  • Does not know familiar smells (e.g., grandma's perfume, coffee shop you frequent).

Interoceptive Domain

Many functions of daily life depend on sensory messages from our body organs.

Some examples include:

  • Soreness you feel after a good abdominal workout.

  • Sensation of being hungry or too full.

  • Sensation of an upset stomach.

  • Sensation of a full bladder.



Postural Disorder

With this type, you have issues perceiving your body's position in space. You may have difficulty stabilizing your body during movement or at rest without core stability.

Tactile-Postural (Surface Interaction)
  • Leans heavily against walls, doorframes, or people when standing in place.

  • Requires constant physical contact with a table, desk, or floor to maintain an upright position.

  • Needs to pull their knees up to their chest or tuck feet under their body to feel stable in a chair.

  • Easily loses balance or falls over when the texture of the floor surface changes unexpectedly.

  • Prefers lying flat on the floor over sitting upright during group or floor-time activities.

Auditory-Postural (Sound-Induced Balance Shifts)
  • Tilts or drops the entire head downward when listening closely to a speaker.

  • Startles so intensely at loud sounds that they physically lose balance or fall over.

  • Sags or slumps their posture significantly when forced to listen to complex or long instructions.

  • Struggles to keep their head upright and stable while tracking the source of a sound.

  • Appears to have worse physical balance and coordination in noisy or loud environments.

Visual-Postural (Visual Anchoring)
  • Slumps down or rests their head on their hand/desk while reading or writing.

  • Loses physical balance completely when closing their eyes to wash their face or change clothes.

  • Locks their gaze firmly onto a fixed object in the room just to stay standing upright.

  • Tends to trip or tilt sideways whenever they look up at the ceiling or the sky.

  • Struggles to maintain a straight sitting or standing posture while tracking a moving object.

Oral-Postural (Core-Jaw Synergy)
  • Sits or stands with an open mouth and slack jaw due to overall low muscle tone.

  • Tenses the neck, jaw, or facial muscles excessively to compensate for a weak, slumping core.

  • Sticks their tongue out or clenches their jaw tightly when performing simple balance tasks.

  • Experiences rapid postural slumping or slouching during meals or while chewing tough foods.

  • Slumps forward instantly whenever their head tilts backward to drink from a cup.

Vestibular & Proprioceptive Postural (Core Muscle Tone)
  • Appears to have "floppy" or weak muscles, frequently slumping heavily over desks or tables.

  • Exhausts easily from brief periods of standing upright, constantly looking for a place to sit.

  • Sits in a "W" position on the floor (knees together, feet flared out wide) to create a wider base.

  • Struggles to stand on one foot or balance on a low curb without immediately falling over.

  • Displays weak arm and leg strength, struggling to push heavy doors or lift basic objects.



Dyspraxia

This version causes a wide range of issues with movement and coordination. It can affect your gross and/or fine motor skills.

Tactile (Touch / Fine Motor Control)
  • Struggles significantly with fine motor tasks like tying shoelaces, buttoning shirts, or using zippers.

  • Holds pencils, crayons, or utensils with an awkward, inefficient, or overly tight grip.

  • Has great difficulty cutting accurately along lines with scissors.

  • Tears paper when erasing or writes with highly inconsistent letter sizes and spacing.

  • Fumbles small objects like coins, keys, or buttons, frequently dropping them.

Auditory-Motor (Rhythm & Timing)
  • Struggles to clap, march, or move their body in sync with a musical beat.

  • Has difficulty following a fast sequence of verbal movement commands (e.g., "jump twice, then turn around").

  • Experiences a noticeable delay between hearing a physical command and starting the movement.

  • Cannot easily mimic a rhythm or pattern tapped out by someone else.

  • Struggles with speech articulation or stuttering due to poor coordination of mouth muscles.

Visual-Spatial Motor (Coordination)
  • Frequently misses, drops, or gets hit by a slow-moving ball when trying to catch it.

  • Struggles to copy shapes, letters, or geometric drawings from a whiteboard onto paper.

  • Frequently misjudges the distance of objects, bumping into tables, doorframes, or walls.

  • Finds it incredibly difficult to pour liquids into a cup without spilling them over the edge.

  • Experiences anxiety or confusion when navigating new physical layouts or crowded spaces.

Oral-Motor (Praxis)
  • Frequently drools, loses food from their mouth, or struggles to use a drinking straw.

  • Moves the entire head or body when trying to move just the tongue or jaw.

  • Struggles to blow bubbles, whistle, or mimic funny facial expressions on command.

  • Finds it difficult to chew complex textures, often pocketing food inside their cheeks.

  • Frequently bites their own tongue, inside of the cheek, or lips accidentally while eating.

Gross Motor Planning (Whole Body)
  • Struggles to learn multi-step physical skills like riding a bike, swimming, or skipping.

  • Appears visibly awkward, rigid, or uncoordinated when running, jumping, or climbing.

  • Cannot easily coordinate the two sides of the body (e.g., pumping legs on a swing).

  • Frequently trips over their own feet or flat surfaces for no apparent reason.

  • Struggles to replicate a dance move or physical pose demonstrated by an instructor.



Recognition and Diagnosis

As SPD isn't yet an official medical condition, it tends to be under-diagnosed due to a lack of precise diagnostic criteria. But healthcare providers are recognizing it — and the benefits of treating it — more and more.



Recognising Sensory Processing Issues in Children

Sensory processing issues are often first recognized during the toddler years, when parents notice that a child has an unusual aversion to noise, light, shoes that are deemed too tight and clothes that are irritating.

They may scream when touched. They may recoil from the textures of certain foods. But others seem unresponsive to anything around them. They may fail to respond to extreme heat or cold or even pain. They may also notice clumsiness and trouble climbing stairs, and difficulty with fine motor skills like wielding a pencil and fastening buttons.

What parents often notice first is odd behaviors and wild mood swings, strange at best, upsetting at worst. Often it's an outsized reaction to a change in environment — a radical, inexplicable shift in the child's behavior.

For instance, a first-grader may do fine in a quiet setting with a calm adult. But place that child in a grocery store filled with an overload of visual and auditory stimulation and you might have the makings of an extreme tantrum, one that's terrifying for both the child and parent.

"These kids' temper tantrums are so intense, so prolonged, so impossible to stop once they've started, you just can't ignore it."

Another response to being overwhelmed is to flee. If a child dashes out across the playground or parking lot, oblivious to the danger, that's a big red flag that he may be heading away from something upsetting or toward an environment or sensation that will calm his system.

Or a child might become aggressive when in sensory overload. They're actually having a neurological "panic" response to everyday sensations the rest of us take for granted.

Some kids on the spectrum are known to wander to water, too often with deadly results. One theory is that water offers input they crave because of sensory issues.

Not all sensory kids do this, but many gravitate toward the sensations and environments they find calming or stimulating. Their self-regulation is not great, so safety takes a back seat to their need to get that input or calming experience.

Some adults may not realise a child has sensory processing issues and negatively interpret their behaviour.

"If you're a child who is oversensitive to certain sensations, you are not only likely to be anxious or irritable, even angry or fearful, you're likely to be called 'picky' and 'oversensitive.'
If you rush away because you're anxious or you're over-stimulated and not using your executive function well because your body has such a powerful need to get away, you're 'impulsive.'
If you have trouble with planning and executing your movements due to poor body awareness and poor organization in the motor areas of the brain, you're 'clumsy.'
Because you're distracted by your sensory issues and trying to make sense of it all, you may be developmentally delayed in some ways, making you a bit 'immature' or young for your age."


Classroom Planning, Schedules, and Routines

  • Have a consistent daily routine.

  • Give advance notice when the routine changes.

  • Build in brain breaks throughout the day.

  • Establish clear starting and ending times for tasks.

  • Post visual schedules, directions, to-do lists, and classroom expectations. Be sure students see them.

  • Use visuals with pictures of sensory input choices. (Print a PDF of options.)



Building Self-Regulation Skills

  • Provide a quiet work space or "calm down" area.

  • Seat the student away from doors, windows, or buzzing lights.

  • Adjust the desk and chair so the student's feet are flat on the floor and hips are at a 90-degree angle. Or put a footstool under the desk.

  • Let the student use alternative seating. Try an exercise ball chair, wobble stool, or stand-up desk.

  • Consult with the occupational therapist (OT) about attaching a stretchy exercise band to the chair legs or desk for students who need to bounce their feet.

  • Let the student work in a different position, like lying on the floor using a clipboard or at an easel.

  • Provide a weighted lap pad, weighted vest, compression vest, air-filled seat cushion, or other OT-approved sensory tools.

  • Provide earplugs or noise-muffling headphones.

  • Let the student use a sensory tool, like a stress ball or a fidget spinner.

  • Have chewing gum available. Or attach a chewable item to the end of a pencil.

  • Let the student sit on a carpet square, in a beanbag chair, or in a chair during group seating.

  • Let the student move as needed within a space outlined in tape or at a seat to the side.

  • Work with the student to come up with nonverbal signals to use when in need of a break.

  • Create a proactive behavior plan for handling sensory triggers.

  • Warn the student when there will be loud noises like bells, announcements, or planned fire alarms.

  • Allow the student to ask for breaks to promote self-regulation.



Giving Instructions and Completing Assignments

  • Reduce the need for handwriting. For example, use fill-in-the-blank questions instead of short-answer questions.

  • Allow extra time for writing if a student has motor skills fatigue or trouble with proprioception.

  • Let the student use speech-to-text software or a computer.

  • Reduce the amount of information on a page.

  • Provide colored overlays for reading to reduce visual distraction.

  • Use blank pieces of paper to cover all but a few of the questions on a page.

  • Use manila folders as a screen to block visual distractions.

  • Offer pencil grips, slant boards, and bold- or raised-line paper for writing.

  • Use a highlighter or sticky notes to help the student stay focused.

  • Allow the student to listen to music while working to keep focused and regulated.
